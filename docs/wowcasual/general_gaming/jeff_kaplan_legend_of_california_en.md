# Jeff Kaplan Unveils "The Legend of California" — His First Game After Blizzard

Jeff "Tigole" Kaplan broke nearly five years of silence with a major announcement on the **Lex Fridman Podcast** (March 2026). His new studio **Kintsugiyama**, a 34-person team featuring Blizzard veteran Tim Ford, has been quietly building a game for the past four years. The result is **The Legend of California** — a multiplayer action-survival FPS set on a mythical island of California during the 19th-century gold rush era, described as "Red Dead Redemption 2 meets Rust."

The game features open-world exploration across distinct biomes and iconic California landmarks. Players gather resources, craft, build ranches with stables and mines, hunt wildlife, and clear hostile encampments — solo or in co-op up to four players on persistent servers. FPS combat emphasizes precision, with optional PvP for territory defense. A procedural seed system reshuffles challenge tiers and points of interest across servers, preventing repetitive play. The art direction draws from 19th-century painter Albert Bierstadt's landscape work.

Published by **Dreamhaven** (founded by ex-Blizzard president Mike Morhaime), the game heads to **public alpha in March 2026**, followed by Early Access on **Steam and Epic Games Store** later in 2026. Kaplan was deliberate about his approach: *"I don't want to crib Blizzard and make a pseudo-Blizzard game"*, emphasizing a distinct identity away from fantasy/sci-fi. He also chose to skip massive marketing, letting the game *"find its audience on its own terms"*, while acknowledging it will be *"the roughest public version of anything he has ever shipped."*

# Why Jeff Kaplan Left Blizzard: Corporate Pressure, OWL, and Broken Trust

In the same Lex Fridman interview, Kaplan revealed for the first time the full story behind his April 2021 departure from Blizzard after 19 years. The core issue was the **Overwatch League** — what he called *"a house of cards."* Executives oversold the league to investors: *"They were pretty much selling the Brooklyn Bridge, that Overwatch League was going to be more popular than the NFL."* Activision projected **$125 million in revenue** that never materialized, and the team was forced to divert resources from game development to support esports monetization, Twitch streaming deals, camera systems, and team skins.

The breaking point came when Blizzard's CFO **Dennis Durkin** gave Kaplan an ultimatum: hit specific revenue targets, or *"we're going to lay off 1,000 people, and that's going to be on you."* Kaplan described this as *"the biggest fuck you moment I've had in my career."* The original Overwatch 2 vision — substantial PVE content alongside PVP — was compromised under corporate pressure to prioritize fast monetization. Where Kaplan once had creative control during Overwatch's early success, OWL and the ballooning scope of OW2 stripped that away.

The emotional toll was immense. Kaplan described separating from Blizzard as *"one of the most painful things"* in his life, saying it *"broke"* him and he only recently recognized the depth of the damage. He urged game developers to *"know their value more and not hand over the golden goose to people who don't deserve it."*
